Lectures And Tutorials
Lectures.

In most cases, the lecturer presents an overview of topics and key issues. These provide good pointers to the important topics you should target in your reading. Lecturers will provide current examples and instructions on preparing for assessment items. Normally, they will initiate discussion and ask questions to encourage you to be actively involved in the lecture. Where possible, use this opportunity to clarify any points you do not fully understand.

Lectures are used to:

  • provide a logical structure in which the significant course topics are covered.
  • provide raw material and information that you can utilise in your course studies.
  • explain key concepts.
  • introduce the most recent research about a topic.
  • indicate where to find further information.
    • Tutorials.

    Tutorials comprise a small group of students and a lecturer or tutor. Their purpose is to review the subject matter for that week in an interactive manner. You may raise questions with your tutor, and clarify confusing issues. Tutorials provide opportunities to explore examples and attempt practice questions. Students could be asked to lead part of a tutorial session, and there may be tutorial tasks that are assessed. The same purposes and principles of interaction operate for tutorial groups conducted online.

    Tutorials provide opportunities to:

  • discuss course material in depth.
  • debate and discuss issues informally.
  • develop and practice the application of course concepts.
  • ask for help or clarification.
  • get to know other students and staff.
    • Science Lab.

    Practicals provide the opportunity for students to observe and manipulate materials and give insight into scientific methods and processes as well as scientific knowledge.

    These resources are intended to develop skills to improve and enhance your writing and communication in Science. Standard ways of communicating scientific information and topics are by writing Lab reports, Annotated bibliographies, Literature reviews, and presenting Posters. Regardless of what type of scientific job you may end up in, effective and accurate communication is important to your studies and highly regarded and sought after by employers.
